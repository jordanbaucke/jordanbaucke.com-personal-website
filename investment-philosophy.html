Investing Philosophy

My interest in financial markets began early, shaped by a family friend's father who encouraged me to take investing seriously rather than treat it as speculation. That curiosity was reinforced by my uncle‚Äîan electrical engineer who built, scaled, and ultimately sold multiple Silicon Valley companies, several of which went on to trade publicly on the American NASDAQ and exit successfully over the course of his career. Watching that arc firsthand made markets feel tangible rather than abstract.

My fascination with equities and, later, derivatives was also influenced by my family's roots in Chicago, where I spent summers growing up. I've long thought of Chicago as the working person's Wall Street‚Äîa city defined by commodity futures and pit-traded derivatives that persisted well into my lifetime. In college, I probably lost more study hours than I should admit sitting in the business school's Bloomberg terminals, pairing a growing understanding of markets with an early love of software by building rudimentary options-trading tools.

I was graduating from college as the Global Financial Crisis reached its depths, an experience that permanently shaped how I think about risk, leverage, and market narratives. Watching once-trusted institutions fail, correlations collapse, and certainty evaporate in real time impressed on me that markets are not purely rational systems‚Äîthey are human systems under stress. That period instilled a lasting skepticism of excess confidence and a deep respect for downside risk, liquidity, and resilience.

I've also been deeply interested in the evolution of market structure and the rise of high-frequency trading, influenced in part by Flash Boys, which framed modern markets as both technologically elegant and structurally fragile.

While my professional career has been in technology, some of my earliest investing lessons came from far more traditional sources‚Äîcountless hours of CNBC in college, including Mad Money with Jim Cramer. One lesson that stuck early is the old adage that the only free lunch in investing is diversification. Because technology is my livelihood, I'm intentionally selective about long-term exposure to tech equities. I prefer not to concentrate my capital in the same sector that already dominates my professional risk, and I remain mindful that a dot-com-style drawdown occurred just before my adult life and investing career began.

While I've read widely on investing and trading, I am not a professional money manager or trader. What the process has given me is a fairly honest understanding of myself‚Äîmy strengths, and more importantly, my weaknesses when it comes to patience, risk tolerance, and conviction. Markets have a way of being very effective mirrors.
